I combine machine learning and linguistics to train models that understand American Sign Language (ASL). My models are trained to discern the systematic relationships between signs' form and meanings, such as recognizing that signs near the eyes convey visual concepts, allowing them to understand signs they haven't seen before. This will be a critical piece in the pursuit of making sign language translators, since datasets are limited with regard to quality and quantity. Beyond sign language, I'm broadly interested in neuro-symbolic learning and sociotechnical systems.

Machine Learning
Machine learning models are trained to recognize patterns in data. Modeling sign language means understanding, translating, and generating sign language data in the form of video or pose estimations. My work focuses on understanding: How can models recognize signs, especially ones they haven't seen before? To achieve this, I use a variety of machine learning techniques, most importantly natural language processing and neuro-symbolic learning.

Natural Language Processing
NLP uses artificial intelligence techniques to understand language. While it commonly focuses on speech or text modality, new large-scale datasets and advanced computer vision techniques have recently enabled NLP for sign language modeling. This sub-field of NLP is tasked with understanding, translating, and generating sign language.

Neuro-Symbolic Learning
In contrast to end-to-end modeling, neuro-symbolic learning provides the model with explicit knowledge and logical reasoning, such as linguistic rules

Sign Language Linguistics
Sign languages are like spoken languages in almost every way. Each sign language has its own vocabulary and grammar, and they are independent of any spoken language used in their region. They have a complex structure at all levels, such as phonology and semantics. My work leverages the relationship between phonology and semantics, also known as systematicity, to help machine learning models understand signs they haven't seen before.

Phonemes
Phonemes are the smallest units of language. In sign languages, they are the handshapes, movements, and locations that make up signs. My paper "Exploring Strategies for Modeling Sign Language Phonology" describes a model which recognizes phonemes in isolated signs with 75–90% accuracy. Read the paper

Morphemes
Morphemes are the smallest meaningful units of language.

Understanding Sign Language
Understanding sign language begins with recognizing signs in all of their forms. My approach focuses on recognizing phonemes and then linking them to meaning. This is a critical step because signs change according to the signer and context, just like speech, and oftentimes the model has never seen !

Sign Recognition
Sign recognition is the task of recognizing signs in video.
